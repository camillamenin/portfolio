ASYLUMCONNECT

ABOUT THE PROJECT

"LGBTQ+ asylum seekers and refugees face unique obstacles during their resettlement journey, from navigating pervasive homophobia and transphobia to social isolation. We are excited to leverage Foursquare's photo data to help visually orient our users so that they are able to physically locate the verified direct service providers listed in AsylumConnect's app. [...] Through joining forces, we can ensure every persecuted LGBTQ+ person has on-demand access to free, trustworthy information for the safe navigation of their new community."


- Jamie Sgarro, AsylumConnect co-founder & executive director

THE PROBLEM

LGBTQ+ asylum seekers belong to a very vulnerable group: they are refugees fleeing persecution due to their sexual orientation or gender identity. When they are forced to migrate to a new country, they often don't have the means, the relationships, or the documents to help them navigate the asylum process.


Asylum Connect's mission is to help them safely resettle in their new home with resources like legal assistance, medical aid, food, and shelters.


THE GOAL

To integrate Foursquare API into AsylumConnect app and website, merging their database of social media and photos in order to better connect and visually orient the users to the organizations listed in AsylumConnect.

– Jacque, the first Mexican to win asylum in the U.S. based on sexual orientation and AsylumConnect Advisory Council member

USERFLOW DESCRIPTION

In order to integrate Foursquare API in AsylumConnect, two user groups were taken into consideration: the volunteers of the Data Entry and Outreach team and the AsylumConnect users. The flowchart was designed considering the goals and the limitations for both volunteers in the Control Panel and users on the Organization page.

FOR ASYLUMCONNECT VOLUNTEERS

The voluteers were the first to choose and approve photos from Foursquare in the Control Panel. The approved photos would then show on the Organization page. This meant that a volunteer needed the ability to perform the following tasks for each organization:


  • Select and approve some or all the photos - the photos from the Foursquare API would first show in an "unapproved photos" section on the Control Panel, and after the verification process the approved ones would move to the "approved photos" section;
  • Deselect and remove some or all the approved photos - as a consequence the photos will move from the "approved photos" section back to the "unapproved photo" section;
  • View a single photo - every photo could be expanded in another window.

FOR ASYLUMCONNECT USERS

On the Organization page the user can see the social media handles directly imported from Foursquare and all the photos that were previously approved by the volunteers. The different use cases depend on who the user is:


  • If the user is the owner, and she has already signed up and claimed the organization, she will be able to make changes to the photos directly on the Organization page, like uploading more photos or deleting existing ones;
  • If the user hasn't signed up yet, a different CTA shows up to invite the user to do so if they are the org owner;
  • If the user has signed up but he's not the organization owner, no message is needed and the user won't be able to make any changes to the photos.

PROJECT ASSIGNMENTS AND DESIGN

After gathering requirements from AsylumConnect's Executive Director, I divided the design work and gave two designers individual ownership of the different user flows: the volunteer's flow in the Control Panel and the user flow on the Organization page.


The design of the Control Panel required the designer to actively communicate with the volunteers in the data entry team to test, iterate, and translate the flow into a usable and clear solution. Given the Control Panel was used internally, we did not focus too much on aesthetics.


The designs on the Organization page were public facing, so great care was taken on both usability and design aesthetics. The designer was given the task to create mockups that responded to users’ needs while complying with AsylumConnect UI system and improving the outdated look of the app.

PROCESS

WHAT I'VE LEARNED

  • To ask the right questions: to listen to the client's needs and requests as well as the user's needs.
  • To start from the user's perspective: to use information architecture to imagine a project's structure first, then its look and feel.
  • To manage a project: to organize the content and keep track of the work progress.
  • To collaborate with my team: to frequently communicate with the other designers, to give them guidelines as well as feedback and support to deliver the project.
  • To collaborate with other teams: to communicate and coordinate with the developers' team and the UX research team for feasibility and usability's best practices.
